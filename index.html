Every equation in physics must be dimensionally consistent: the left side and the right side must carry the same combination of mass, length, time, and any other base quantity. This isn't a technicality — it is a constraint so powerful that it can tell you the shape of formulas before you derive them.

We use the symbols [M] for mass, [L] for length, [T] for time, and [Θ] for temperature. In this worksheet we will determine the dimensions of each of the following constants — then combine them to discover a fundamental length scale at the frontier of physics.

Prerequisite Skills: Exponents & Simultaneous Equations

Before we touch any physics, let's make sure two algebraic tools are sharp. You'll use both on every page that follows.

The laws of exponents

Dimensional analysis treats units as algebraic symbols raised to powers. The rules below are the complete toolkit you need.

Multiplication
\(x^a \cdot x^b = x^{a+b}\)
Division
\(\dfrac{x^a}{x^b} = x^{a-b}\)
Power of a power
\((x^a)^n = x^{an}\)
Negative exponent
\(x^{-a} = \dfrac{1}{x^a}\)
Fractional exponent
\(x^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{x}\)
Zero exponent
\(x^0 = 1\)

Example with units. Velocity has dimensions \(\text{L}\,\text{T}^{-1}\). Acceleration is \(\text{L}\,\text{T}^{-2}\). Their ratio is:

\(\dfrac{[\text{velocity}]}{[\text{acceleration}]} = \dfrac{\text{L}\,\text{T}^{-1}}{\text{L}\,\text{T}^{-2}} = \text{L}^{1-1}\,\text{T}^{-1-(-2)} = \text{T}^{1} = \text{T}\)

— the dimensions of time. This makes physical sense: velocity ÷ acceleration = time.

Solving simultaneous equations — by algebra and by graph

A pair of linear equations in two unknowns can be solved in two complementary ways. You should be comfortable with both.

Method 1 — Algebraic elimination. Given:

\(2x + y = 7 \qquad \text{and} \qquad -x + 3y = 8\)

Multiply the second equation by 2 to make the \(x\)-coefficients match in magnitude:

\(-2x + 6y = 16\)

Add to the first: \((2x - 2x) + (y + 6y) = 7 + 16\), giving \(7y = 23\), so \(y = 23/7\). Back-substitute: \(x = (7 - 23/7)/2 = 13/7\).

Method 2 — Graphical (coordinate) interpretation. Each equation is a straight line on the \(xy\)-plane. Their intersection point is the solution.

Two lines meet at a single point — the solution to both equations. Every simultaneous-equation problem in this worksheet can be pictured this way.

Part A

The Main Idea: Homogeneity of Dimensions

Why units must balance — the period of an oscillating spring

Hang a mass \(m\) from a spring, pull it down, and let go. It bounces up and down with a period \(T\) — the time for one complete oscillation. Experiments show that the period depends on the mass and the spring constant \(k\) (a measure of stiffness, in newtons per metre). The formula is:

\(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}}\)

Let's check this using dimensional analysis. The principle of homogeneity says: both sides of any valid equation must have the same dimensions.

The left side has dimensions \([\,T\,] = \text{T}\) (time). Let's check the right. First: what are the dimensions of the spring constant \(k\)?

Since \(F = kx\), with \([F] = \text{M}\text{L}\text{T}^{-2}\) and \([x] = \text{L}\):

\([k] = \dfrac{[F]}{[x]} = \dfrac{\text{M}\text{L}\text{T}^{-2}}{\text{L}} = \text{M}\text{T}^{-2}\)

Now check the right side of the period formula:

\(\left[\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}}\right] = \left(\dfrac{\text{M}}{\text{M}\text{T}^{-2}}\right)^{1/2} = \left(\text{T}^{2}\right)^{1/2} = \text{T}\quad\checkmark\)

Both sides give \(\text{T}\). The equation is dimensionally consistent. Notice that the \(2\pi\) is invisible to dimensional analysis — it's a pure number. Dimensional analysis gets the shape of the formula but not numerical coefficients.

Part B

Hot Objects Glow: The Stefan–Boltzmann Law

The story of thermal radiation

Hold your hand near a campfire and you feel heat — not from the air, but from invisible infrared radiation streaming out of the flames. Every warm object radiates energy: the coils of a toaster glow red, the filament of an old light-bulb glows white, and even your own body radiates in the infrared, which is how thermal cameras see you in the dark.

In 1879, Josef Stefan measured how much total power a hot object radiates and found a striking rule: the power per unit area goes as the fourth power of temperature. His student Ludwig Boltzmann later derived the result from thermodynamics:

\(\dfrac{P}{A} = \sigma\, T^4\)

Here \(P\) is radiated power (watts), \(A\) is surface area, \(T\) is absolute temperature in kelvin, and \(\sigma\) is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant. The fourth-power dependence is dramatic: double the temperature and the radiated power leaps by a factor of \(2^4 = 16\). This is why a steel furnace at 2000 K vastly out-glows a warm radiator at 350 K.

Recall: Power = energy per time, so \([P] = [\text{energy}]/[\text{time}] = \text{M}\text{L}^2\text{T}^{-3}\). You will need this.

Part C

Molecules in Motion: The Gas Law & Boltzmann's Constant

From steam engines to fundamental constants

The ideal gas law connects pressure, volume, and temperature:

\(PV = nRT = Nk_B T\)

Here \(n\) is the number of moles, \(R\) is the universal gas constant, \(N\) is the total number of molecules (a pure number — no units), and \(k_B\) is the Boltzmann constant. Boltzmann's constant bridges the macroscopic world of pressure gauges and the microscopic world of individual molecules: the average kinetic energy of a single molecule is \(\tfrac{3}{2}\,k_B T\).

Since \(N\) is dimensionless, the product \(Nk_B T\) must have the same dimensions as \(PV\). This constraint alone pins down the units of \(k_B\).

Part D

Light from Atoms: The Rydberg Constant

Why hydrogen glows in colour

Pass an electric current through a tube of hydrogen gas and it glows with a distinctive pink-violet light. Viewed through a prism, this glow breaks into a series of sharp, discrete lines at very specific wavelengths — not a continuous rainbow. In 1888, Johannes Rydberg discovered that every wavelength obeyed a single formula:

\(\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_\infty\!\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

Here \(\lambda\) is the wavelength, \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are positive integers with \(n_2 > n_1\), and \(R_\infty\) is the Rydberg constant. The bracket is a pure number, so the dimensions of \(R_\infty\) are entirely determined by what sits on the left side.

Part E

The Force Between Worlds: Newton's \(G\)

Gravity and the gravitational constant

Newton's law of universal gravitation says that every pair of masses attracts each other:

\(F = \dfrac{G\,M\,m}{r^2}\)

Here \(F\) is force, \(M\) and \(m\) are two masses, \(r\) is the distance between their centres, and \(G\) is the gravitational constant. \(G\) is extraordinarily small — about \(6.67 \times 10^{-11}\) in SI units — which is why gravity is by far the weakest of the fundamental forces. You need planet-sized masses to feel it strongly.

Two masses attracting each other. The force depends on both masses and the square of the distance. Your job: find the dimensions of \(G\).

Part F

Quanta of Light: Planck's Constant \(h\)

The birth of quantum mechanics

In 1900, Max Planck resolved the "ultraviolet catastrophe" — classical physics' prediction that hot objects should radiate infinite energy at short wavelengths — by proposing that light energy comes in discrete packets:

\(E = hf\)

Here \(f\) is the frequency of light (oscillations per second) and \(h\) is Planck's constant — the fundamental quantum of action. It is tiny, about \(6.63 \times 10^{-34}\;\text{J·s}\), which is why quantum effects are invisible in everyday life. Notice the connection to Part B: Planck's hypothesis was invented precisely to explain the Stefan–Boltzmann law and the shape of the thermal radiation spectrum.

Part G

The Expanding Universe: Hubble's Law

Every galaxy is racing away from us

In 1929, Edwin Hubble made one of the most profound discoveries in science. By measuring light from distant galaxies, he found that their spectral lines were shifted to the red — they were moving away from us. More astonishingly, the further away a galaxy was, the faster it receded:

\(v = H_0\,d\)

Here \(v\) is the recession velocity, \(d\) is the distance to the galaxy, and \(H_0\) is the Hubble constant. This means the universe is expanding — space itself is stretching.

Part H

When Light Cannot Escape: Black Holes & the Planck Length

From escape velocity to the event horizon

Throw a ball upward and gravity pulls it back. Throw it faster and it goes higher. At a critical speed — the escape velocity — the ball never returns. For the Earth's surface, this is about 11.2 km/s.

Now imagine compressing an object until its escape velocity reaches the speed of light, \(c \approx 3 \times 10^8\;\text{m/s}\). Beyond that point, nothing — not even light — can escape. The boundary is called the event horizon, and the object inside is a black hole.

Quantum meets gravity: the Compton wavelength

Quantum mechanics assigns a wavelength to every particle. From Einstein's \(E = mc^2\) and Planck's \(E = hf = hc/\lambda\), equating gives the Compton wavelength:

\(\lambda_C \sim \dfrac{h}{mc}\)

This is the length scale at which quantum effects dominate for a particle of mass \(m\).

Task H2

Now multiply the Schwarzschild radius by the Compton wavelength for the same mass (\(M = m\)):

  2. Verify that \([Gh/c^3] = \text{L}^2\) by substituting dimensions.
  3. The square root defines the Planck length, \(\ell_P = \sqrt{Gh/c^3} \approx 10^{-35}\;\text{m}\). This is the scale where quantum gravity effects dominate.

Part I

Geometry Meets Algebra: The Unit Circle & Straight Lines

Where circles meet lines

The unit circle is the set of all points at distance 1 from the origin:

\(x^2 + y^2 = 1\)

Every point on it can be written \((\cos\theta,\;\sin\theta)\). A straight line in slope-intercept form is \(y = mx + c\). Finding where these meet is a simultaneous-equation problem: substitute one into the other and solve the resulting quadratic. The number of solutions (0, 1, or 2) tells you whether the line misses, is tangent to, or cuts through the circle.

This connects directly to physics: finding where two trajectories meet (rocket intercepts planet) is the same algebra.

The line \(y = x\) intersects the unit circle at two points. Finding them requires solving a pair of simultaneous equations.

Part J

The Geometric Mean: Midpoints on a Log Scale

Bridging vastly different scales

The ordinary (arithmetic) mean of 1 and 100 is 50.5 — biased heavily toward the large number. But on a logarithmic scale, the midpoint between 1 and 100 is 10, because \(\log(1) = 0\), \(\log(100) = 2\), and the midpoint is \(\log = 1 \to 10^1 = 10\). This is the geometric mean:

\(\text{geometric mean of } A \text{ and } B = \sqrt{A \times B}\)

In physics, where quantities span dozens of orders of magnitude, the geometric mean is a far more natural "middle ground."

\(10^0 = 1\)
\(10^1 = \mathbf{10}\)
\(10^2 = 100\)
On a log scale, 10 sits exactly halfway between 1 and 100.
